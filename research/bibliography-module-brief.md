# research and bibliography modules: brief

A brief for the colleague building the Canvas bibliography modules. It states what I expect the research and bibliography work to accomplish in each seminar. The module design and the tasks are yours; the outcomes below are what I am after.

## the situation

We used to teach research and bibliography as its own course. We are folding it into the two analysis seminars instead, one module in 601, one in 602. I want the two to run in parallel but not identically, because the papers they support differ in kind, and the research tasks have to follow the papers.

## where the students start

Assume no prior research training. They cannot yet find scholarly sources, tell a strong one from a weak one, or cite anything correctly. Many have never opened RILM or a critical edition, and cannot tell a primary source from a secondary one. Both modules build from that floor. The 601 module builds it first. The 602 module cannot assume a student took 601, so it has to build the same floor again, faster.

## the shared foundation

Both modules teach the same core research literacy from zero. A student who finishes either one should be able to:

- Tell primary sources (scores and critical editions, composer writings and letters, contemporaneous documents, recordings) from secondary ones (scholarly books, journal articles, dissertations, reference entries), and scholarly sources from everything else.
- Judge what makes a source trustworthy: peer review, the author's standing, the venue or publisher, currency, and, specific to music, whether an edition or a recording is authoritative. Which edition you cite is a scholarly decision.
- Find sources with the tools of the field: RILM, JSTOR, Oxford Music Online and Grove, the library catalog and WorldCat, ProQuest Dissertations, and IMSLP and critical editions for scores. Move from a topic to search terms, and follow the footnotes of a good source to further sources.
- Cite in Chicago, notes-bibliography, the norm for music scholarship: footnotes and bibliography entries for the source types we actually use, including scores and editions, book chapters, journal articles, dissertations, liner notes, and online reference works.
- Write an annotated entry that summarizes the source, judges it, and says what it is good for.

## what differs, and why

The papers drive the difference. In 601 the papers are short and analytical, close readings of the music that hold historical and interpretive context to a brief frame. The bibliography there is lean and in service of the analysis. In 602 the work culminates in a full research paper, developed and revised across the term, where the scholarly context is the point. The bibliography there is the backbone of the paper.

## 601: what I expect

The 601 module trains the mechanics and the habits at small scale, repeated across the five analytical papers. For these papers a student needs to do two things well and often: identify and cite the authoritative score or edition of the work being analyzed, and find and cite the focused analytical or theoretical literature that grounds the reading. The volume is small on purpose. I would rather they get the score citation and two solid secondary sources exactly right, five times, than assemble a long list they cannot evaluate.

Tie the tasks to the papers. A short source exercise attached to a paper works better here than a standalone unit: locate the right edition, find one or two scholarly sources that bear on the analysis, evaluate them, cite them correctly. By the end they should be fluent in primary-source (score and edition) literacy and clean Chicago citation, and comfortable finding a small, relevant set of secondary sources.

## 602: what I expect

The 602 module carries the full research arc, because the paper does. It runs alongside the iterative paper sequence: research question, then a graded annotated bibliography, then draft, revision, and final, plus the conference-style presentation. The annotated bibliography is a real milestone, not a warm-up. Students build and annotate a substantial set of scholarly sources (on the order of eight to twelve, your call) and use it to place their own work in the scholarly conversation.

Two things go deeper here than in 601. First, critical evaluation: post-tonal theory is itself evolving and contested, so students have to weigh competing analytical frameworks and take a position rather than collect sources that agree. Second, synthesis: the paper connects analysis to historical and cultural context and enters an existing conversation, which means reading sources against each other rather than one at a time.

## sequencing

Read as a year, 601 lays the foundation and 602 builds the research paper on it, so a student who takes both should feel escalation, not repetition. Because they are also independent courses, each module stands on its own: 602 reteaches the foundation quickly for students who did not take 601, then moves past it.

## fixed and open

Fixed: Chicago, notes-bibliography, throughout, and the tie to the papers as above. Open and yours: the module structure, the tasks and their sequencing in Canvas, the tutorials and worked examples, and how much is graded. For reference, the 601 paper guidelines are in the course repo at `research/analytical-papers.md`, and I can share the syllabus and the paper prompts so you can see exactly what the bibliography has to support.
